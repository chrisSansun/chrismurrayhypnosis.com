Can You Be Hypnotised?

A lot of people ask me this before their first session. The short answer: almost certainly yes. Can you be hypnotised depends far less on mystical ability or stubbornness than most people think. It's not about willpower or how suggestible you are. It's actually about attention, curiosity, and a willingness to relax for an hour. Most people can enter hypnosis easily once they understand what they're actually signing up for.

What Actually Determines Hypnotisability

Research into hypnotic susceptibility, also called hypnotisability, shows something surprising: the average person scores in the moderate to high range on standardized tests. About 85% of people are capable of entering trance at some level. That's not 85% of naturally gifted people. That's 85% of the general population.

Hypnotisability doesn't correlate strongly with intelligence, age, gender, or how "strong-willed" you are. In fact, people who are good at focusing, visualization, and absorption - think artists, academics, athletes - often hypnotize more easily. Why? Because hypnosis isn't about being weak. It's a state of focused attention. You're not being controlled. You're directing your mind toward a specific purpose.

The factors that do matter are context and expectation. If you're skeptical but willing, you'll likely hypnotize fine. If you're desperate for it to work and tense the entire time, you'll struggle. If your hypnotherapist creates safety and explains what's actually happening, your chances improve significantly.

Why You Might Think You Can't Be Hypnotised

Common myths about hypnosis are partly to blame. People expect something theatrical - eyes rolling back, sudden trance, no memory afterward. Real hypnosis feels different. Many people say they weren't hypnotized because they could hear the therapist's voice or think during it. That's exactly what happens in hypnosis.

Some people confuse hypnosis with meditation or sleep. It's neither. You're conscious. You can reject suggestions. You can move or talk if you need to. Your brain is simply in a state of relaxed focus, similar to being absorbed in a book or driving on autopilot.

Others worry they'll "lose control." That fear itself creates tension, which makes deep relaxation harder. Understanding that you're always in control - that you're actually choosing to follow suggestions because they align with your goals - usually resolves this. The Role of Belief (And Why It Matters Less Than You Think)

Belief helps. It removes resistance. But it's not essential. Studies show people with low expectation can still enter hypnosis if the therapist is skilled and the framing is right. You don't need to believe hypnosis works. You need to be willing to try it and pay attention for an hour. That's genuinely it.

What matters more is the therapeutic relationship. Do you trust your hypnotherapist? Do they explain the process in a way that makes sense to you? Are they meeting you where you are, not pushing you into some imagined state? Good practitioners adjust their approach based on your learning style and concerns, which makes hypnosis more accessible to more people.

The expectation that can hurt you is the one where hypnosis solves everything instantly. Hypnosis is a tool. It's powerful for anxiety, confidence, habit change, and focus. But it works alongside your own motivation and effort, not instead of it. Your first hypnotherapy session will set clear expectations about what hypnosis can and can't do for your specific situation.

Who Actually Struggles

Some people do find hypnosis harder. Active resistance is real - if you've decided it won't work before you start, that protective skepticism can be tough to override. Certain medications can affect the relaxation response. Extreme stress or untreated trauma sometimes makes it harder to settle, though hypnotherapy itself can address these. And if someone has neurological differences that affect attention or sensory processing, the approach might need adapting.

But "struggles" doesn't mean impossible. It usually means skilled hypnotherapy becomes more important, not less. A practitioner trained in multiple induction methods - not just one standard script - can find what works for your brain.

Key Takeaways

  • About 85% of people are moderately to highly hypnotizable - far more than most assume.
  • Can you be hypnotised depends on attention and willingness, not willpower or natural gift.
  • You stay conscious and in control during hypnosis - you hear everything and can reject suggestions.
  • Myths about hypnosis create unnecessary fear; understanding the real process often resolves hesitation.
  • Trust in your hypnotherapist and clear expectations matter more than natural ability.
