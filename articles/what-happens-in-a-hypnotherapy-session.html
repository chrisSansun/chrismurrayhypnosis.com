What Happens During a Hypnotherapy Session?

A lot of people come to me asking what happens in hypnotherapy, and they're often surprised by the answer. There's no swinging watch. No moment where you suddenly go blank. What actually happens in a hypnotherapy session is far more straightforward, and honestly, far more useful. You'll be awake the entire time, aware of everything around you, in control. The goal isn't to put you under - it's to help your mind work differently so you can change patterns that aren't serving you.

The Opening Conversation

Every session starts the same way: we talk. I want to understand what's brought you in, what you've already tried, and what success actually looks like for you. This isn't small talk. I'm gathering information about your history, your triggers, and the specific ways your mind tends to get stuck. Most people find this part relaxing simply because someone's actually listening - not judging, not jumping to solutions, just understanding the shape of your problem.

We'll discuss your expectations too. Some clients think hypnotherapy works like a magic switch. Others are deeply skeptical. Both are fine. Belief isn't a prerequisite, but willingness is. I need to know you're genuinely willing to engage with the process, even if you don't fully understand it yet. This is also when I explain what hypnosis actually is, cutting through the Hollywood nonsense so you know what to expect.

Induction and Deepening

Once we've talked through everything, I'll guide you into what's called the inductive state. This sounds technical, but it's really just a guided relaxation. I'll ask you to focus on something - your breathing, a point in the room, or an imagined scene. Your eyes typically close, your muscles relax, and your conscious mind steps back a bit. You're not asleep. You're not unconscious. You're in a state where your thinking mind is quieter and your receptive mind is more active.

The deepening phase follows. I'll use specific language and techniques to help you move deeper into this focused, receptive state. Your body might feel heavier or lighter. You might notice your breathing slow. This is all normal. Some people describe it as being aware but not caring about the outside world. Others say it's like daydreaming except you know exactly where you are. The variation is huge - hypnosis looks different for everyone.

The Therapeutic Work

Now we get to the actual work. This is where cognitive hypnotherapy differs from traditional hypnosis. Rather than simple suggestions ("you will never want to smoke"), I work with the patterns your mind has built. We explore the beliefs underlying your behaviours. We identify the function that symptom is serving - what does anxiety protect you from? What does procrastination allow you to avoid? Once we understand that, we can work with it differently.

I might use metaphors, visualisations, or direct suggestion depending on what's relevant for you. The key is that your deeper mind is listening in this state. We're bypassing the critical conscious mind that often resists change or over-analyses. Research shows that suggestions and insights accepted during hypnosis have a stronger lasting effect than simply talking about change in ordinary conversation. Your mind is in a state where it's genuinely more open to new patterns, new responses, new possibilities.

Emergence and Integration

When we've done the therapeutic work, I'll guide you back to full wakefulness. This happens gradually, never abruptly. I might count upwards or describe you returning your awareness to the room. You'll open your eyes, feel refreshed, and be completely aware of everything that just happened. You'll remember the key points we worked on - this isn't amnesia. You're simply returning to your normal conscious state with new resources accessible to you.

The final part of the session is integration. We talk about what happened, what you noticed, and how this work will develop over the coming days. Sometimes the shift is immediate. Sometimes it unfolds over a week or two as your mind processes and applies what we've done. Most people find that returning for another session extends and deepens the work - this isn't normally a one-session fix for complex patterns. That's honest, not disappointing. Real change takes engagement, and multiple sessions give your mind more opportunity to cement new ways of responding.

Key Takeaways

  • You're conscious and in control throughout a hypnotherapy session - it's guided relaxation, not unconsciousness.
  • The opening conversation is essential, not filler - understanding your specific pattern is what makes the work effective.
  • Cognitive hypnotherapy works with the beliefs and functions underlying your behaviours, not just surface suggestions.
  • Integration happens gradually after the session as your mind applies new patterns in real life.
  • Multiple sessions typically deepen results because sustained engagement creates lasting neurological change.
