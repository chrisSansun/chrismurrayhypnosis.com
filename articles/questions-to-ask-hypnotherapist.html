Questions to Ask Before Booking a Hypnotherapist

Booking your first hypnotherapy session can feel daunting if you don't know what to ask. Most practitioners offer an initial consultation, and this is your chance to assess whether they're the right fit. Knowing what questions to ask a hypnotherapist makes the difference between working with someone confident and qualified, or wasting time with someone who isn't equipped to help. This article lists the essential questions that separate competent practitioners from the rest.

Questions About Qualifications and Training

Start here. Ask: what qualifications do you hold? Where did you train? How long did your training take? A legitimate hypnotherapist should be able to rattle off their credentials without hesitation. Look for answers like "I'm a Dip.C.Hyp from Quest Institute" or "I'm ASCH-certified with an NLP diploma." Avoid vague answers like "I've been doing this for years" or "I'm naturally gifted." Next ask: are you registered with any professional bodies? In the UK, BSCAH or NCH registration is the gold standard. In the US, ASCH is reliable. Any legitimate practitioner will have professional registration because it demonstrates they meet training standards and adhere to ethical guidelines. Ask when they last did continuing professional development. Clinical hypnotherapy evolves. A practitioner trained 15 years ago who hasn't done formal training since is potentially working with outdated information. Good practitioners invest in ongoing development. Finally, ask: do you carry professional indemnity insurance? This protects you if something goes wrong. If they don't, that's a significant red flag.

Questions About Their Approach

Ask: what's your primary approach or modality? Different practitioners use different frameworks - cognitive hypnotherapy, Ericksonian hypnotherapy, NLP-based work, solution-focused hypnotherapy, or combinations thereof. None is objectively better, but they're different, and you should know which you're getting. Follow up by asking: how would you describe what you do in simple terms? A good practitioner can translate their jargon into plain English. If they can't, or if they seem annoyed by the question, that's telling. Ask: how does your approach work? What's the theory behind it? They should be able to explain not just what they do but why they believe it works. This helps you understand whether their framework makes sense to you. Finally, ask: do you adapt your approach to different clients, or do you use the same method for everyone? A good answer is "I adapt" because different people respond to different techniques.

Questions About Your Specific Issue

Ask: have you worked with my specific issue before? If they say yes, ask them to describe their typical approach and what kind of results they see. Be specific. Don't just accept "I work with anxiety" - ask how many anxiety clients they've treated, what types of anxiety (social anxiety, generalised anxiety, performance anxiety are different), and what their typical success rate is. Ask: what do you think is driving my issue? A good practitioner, even at the consultation stage, should have some insight based on your description. They might say something like, "Often with performance anxiety, the nervous system has learned to interpret the performance context as a threat. We'd typically work on updating that threat response." A practitioner who hasn't formed any hypothesis yet, or who gives you generic advice, probably hasn't thought deeply about your specific issue. Follow up by asking: what would success look like? Help them articulate a clear outcome. If you can't both agree on what success looks like, you'll struggle to measure progress.

Questions About Session Structure and Logistics

Ask: how long are sessions? Standard sessions are typically 60 to 90 minutes. Ask: what happens in the first session? You should expect a consultation phase before any hypnotic work. Ask: how often will we meet? Weekly is typical for most people, though some practitioners work fortnightly or monthly depending on the issue. Ask: what's your cancellation policy? Some practitioners charge if you cancel with less than 24 hours notice. That's reasonable. Others might be more flexible. Ask: do you offer sessions online or in person? If online, what platform do you use? They should be using a secure, professional platform. If they're vague about technology, be cautious. Ask: can I record sessions? Most practitioners say no, which is fine, but it's worth confirming upfront what their policy is.

Questions About Cost and Number of Sessions

Ask: what's your fee per session? Ask: do you offer package discounts if I commit to multiple sessions? Many practitioners offer 10% to 20% discounts for packages of 6 or more sessions. Ask: roughly how many sessions will I need? A good practitioner should give an informed estimate based on your issue and their experience. Be wary of guarantees - no one can promise you'll be "fixed" in a specific number of sessions. But they should be honest. For something like a simple habit, they might say 3 to 5 sessions. For complex anxiety, they might say 6 to 12. Ask: what happens if I progress faster or slower than expected? A good practitioner builds flexibility into their approach and can adjust the timeline if needed. Finally, ask: what's your refund or guarantee policy? If it's very vague or non-existent, that's a concern. At minimum, you should feel confident that if you're not connecting after the first session, you can say so and either adjust your approach or part ways.

Questions About What You'll Do Between Sessions

Ask: what should I do between sessions? Good practitioners typically give you homework or practices to do between sessions. This might be guided visualisation recordings to listen to, journaling to do, or specific behaviours to notice. The homework accelerates progress. If a practitioner says "just come back next week, we'll handle it all in the sessions," they're probably not maximising your outcomes. Ask: will you provide recordings or resources? Many good practitioners send clients recordings they can use daily between sessions. These anchor the work and speed up progress. Ask: what if something comes up between sessions? Clarify whether they're available for emergency contact or whether they have protocols for crisis situations. Most practitioners have boundaries around emergency contact, which is fine, but you should know what to do if something urgent arises.

Questions That Reveal Character

Ask: what's your success rate? This is tricky because it depends on how you define success and what issues you're measuring. But a good practitioner should have thought about this and have some framework for measuring outcomes. Ask: when have you failed with a client? A practitioner who says "never" is either being dishonest or not working with the kinds of complex issues that sometimes don't resolve. Ask: who shouldn't work with you? Good practitioners know their limits. They might say, "I'm not the right person for someone with severe, untreated bipolar disorder - I'd want them to have psychiatric support first." This honesty is a sign of competence and ethics. Ask: what do you do if I don't feel like we're making progress? A good practitioner takes this seriously and either adjusts approach or helps you find someone better suited. This reveals whether they're genuinely invested in your outcome or just collecting fees.

The Questions You Should Ask Yourself

After your consultation, reflect: Did they listen more than they talked? Did they ask probing questions about your situation? Did they explain things in a way I understood? Did I feel respected? Do I trust them? These subjective impressions matter. What to expect in your first hypnotherapy session partly depends on the quality of relationship you've built in the consultation. Trust and rapport are therapeutic factors in themselves. If you're not feeling a connection, keep looking. It's also worth asking them: what do you need from me to do my best work? A good practitioner will say something like "I need you to be honest, to do the work between sessions, and to tell me if something isn't working." This clarity about roles and responsibilities matters. Finally, if after your consultation you're still uncertain, consider whether hypnotherapy is right for you at all, or whether you simply need a different practitioner.

Key Takeaways

  • Ask about qualifications, training length, professional registration, and continuing professional development - these reveal whether they're genuinely qualified.
  • Ask them to explain their approach in simple terms and how they'll adapt it specifically for your issue.
  • Ask what success will look like, what their typical session structure is, and roughly how many sessions you'll need.
  • Ask about costs, package discounts, cancellation policies, and what homework or practices you'll do between sessions.
  • Ask about their success rate, when they've failed, and what types of clients aren't suitable for their approach.
  • Trust your instinct - a good practitioner will make you feel heard, respected, and confident in their competence.
